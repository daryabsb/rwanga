## Compressed Narrative

### Opening

The film opens with a montage of disturbing flash-forwards: someone digging a hole, a doll burning, a body being carried, a car falling off a cliff, a car accident inside a tunnel with police. These images will make sense by the end.

### Act 1 — The Setup (Scenes 1–11)

**Nali**, a 38-year-old playwright and theater director, wakes up late in his garden house outside Sulaymaniyah. His art director **Nazi** calls — she and **Adib** are coming tomorrow. Nali watches from his garden as an ambulance arrives at the neighbor's house across the hill. The gardener tells him a young couple was found dead — possibly murdered, not a gas leak as first reported. Police are checking all security cameras in the area.

That evening, Nali video-calls **Gesha**, a young woman he met online two months ago. She's wearing a green herbal face mask. They have a lively, flirtatious conversation about masks — the ones people wear in society. Nali wants to postpone their meeting because of the police activity nearby, but Gesha insists: "If you delay, I'll never come." They agree to meet the next day before sunset. After hanging up, Nali calls his **AI assistant** — a habit he's developed as a substitute for human connection. The AI tells him to stop reading people's minds and just meet her.

The next day, Nali drives to pick up Gesha. He stops for a hitchhiking **woman and child** — the woman mentions a "tragic incident" the day before, making Nali uneasy. He passes through a **police checkpoint** with extra security. He meets Gesha at the **Sulaymaniyah market** — she's wearing a floral hat and sunglasses. She's standing by a flower seller, hoping Nali will buy her flowers. Instead, Nali secretly calls AI to complain about women who need attention. AI tells him not to overthink it.

They drive together. Nali stops at a sweet shop and buys **Shrubi** (traditional syrup sweets) that Gesha says she hasn't had in years and craves desperately. *[NOTE: This is the murder weapon the audience doesn't recognize yet — Gesha has diabetes, and excessive sugar will trigger the heart attack that kills her.]* Gesha is strangely quiet and stressed. She refuses to get out of the car at a fruit stand, seems nervous about being seen. She secretly checks her phone — messages she doesn't open — then turns it off.

Nali challenges Gesha to count the lights in an upcoming tunnel for a chance to act in his play. She counts with infectious joy — one of the few moments of pure happiness in the film. At **another checkpoint**, a soldier warns them about a drunk killer loose in the area. Gesha becomes frightened. Nali tries to calm her by telling a story about a woman in Paris with yellow hair who drank coffee and stared at him.

They arrive at the garden. Nali's dog **Gula** greets them warmly. Gesha is charmed by the dog and the beauty of the place.

### Act 2A — The Evening (Scenes 12–18)

**Scene 12** is the turning point: Gesha enters the garden house and is shocked by the interior — taxidermy heads, theatrical masks, daggers, alien sculptures. It looks like a mad artist's museum. She discovers a paper on Nali's desk reading "Stories are waiting" — the title of his new play. She tries a locked door that won't open. Nali deflects all her questions casually. She goes to change clothes.

Nali feeds the dog and consults AI about how to build trust with Gesha. AI suggests asking about her dreams. Gesha returns in a short floral dress. They cook dinner together. Nali starts a game of **Truth or Dare** with a spinning bottle. The game is light at first but escalates into increasingly personal territory.

Key game moments: Gesha asks if Nali would stay with someone who has a terminal illness (he says yes, not knowing she's really asking about herself). Nali gets angry when Gesha dares him to show her his phone. He demands to know why she came if she doesn't trust him. The tension peaks when Gesha asks "Do you want this relationship to be serious?" and Nali responds that he can't commit until he fully knows someone. The game reveals both characters: Nali is guarded by past betrayals, Gesha is desperate for love but hiding her own secrets.

Gesha eats the Shrubi sweets excessively — smearing them on her face like a performer, dancing, laughing wildly. It's joyful but unhinged. She declares she'll sleep on the sofa. *[The audience doesn't know it yet, but this sugar binge is killing her.]* In his room, Nali calls AI and admits he likes Gesha but still doesn't fully trust her. He falls asleep repeating her words: "Even if your ceiling was the sky, I'd still be with you."

**Dare sequence (Scene 17):** Gesha must walk to the far end of the dark garden and turn on the lights. She goes alone through the dark trees. When she returns running, Nali is inside — he has secretly gone through her bag and tried to open her phone (it was off). He then scares her with the deer head prop. They laugh, but something has shifted.

### Act 2B — The Crisis (Scenes 19–29)

**Scene 19** is the horror pivot. Nali wakes up and finds Gesha dead on the sofa. She's cold, hasn't been breathing for hours. He panics. He calls Nazi — she and the others are almost there. He calls AI and pretends he's "writing a story" about a man who wakes up to find a dead woman. AI plays along but notices his breathing is real. The AI suggests: hide the body somewhere in the house before the friends arrive.

Nali stuffs Gesha's body into the hollow compartment of the **sofa** — the same sofa she chose to sleep on. He hides her phone and belongings inside too. His phone rings — Nazi says they're at the gate.

**Nazi, Adib, Ako, and Tara arrive** (Scenes 20–23). Nali is barely holding together. Nazi notices leftover Shrubi and flour on the table. Everyone sits on and around the sofa containing Gesha's body. The group is there to rehearse **Chekhov's "The Seagull"** — a play within the film about art, love, and self-destruction.

The rehearsal becomes its own drama. **Adib** (40, the lead actor) is drunk and hostile toward **Tara** (the lead actress) because she's had cosmetic surgery and he feels her face has lost its natural expression. Nali is distracted, staring at the sofa. He secretly calls AI asking how to make everyone leave. AI's advice: "Cause a fight."

**Dinner scene** — Adib drinks heavily. They discuss the neighbor's murder. Nali encourages the drinking to speed up their departure. During a rehearsal of "The Seagull," Adib stands on the sofa (Gesha's sofa) to grab a stuffed bird from the wall. Nali panics. Adib insults Tara's acting and appearance. Tara leaves in anger. Ako and Nazi follow. Nazi warns Nali: don't let Adib drive.

**Adib refuses to leave** (Scene 28). He insists on sleeping on the sofa. Nali begs him to use the bedroom instead, but Adib stubbornly clings to the sofa, trying to open it up to sleep on. Nali stays awake all night watching him.

**Scene 29 — The Second Death.** Morning. Adib wakes up, reaches under the sofa for water, and discovers Gesha's body. Nali, in blind panic, pushes Adib. Adib falls and hits his head on a stone sculpture. He dies instantly. Nali now has two bodies.

### Act 3 — The Cover-Up and Revelation (Scenes 30–43)

Nali drives Adib's car with Adib's body in the passenger seat to a remote valley and pushes the car off a cliff — staging it as a drunk driving accident. He returns home and reviews the **security camera footage**: he sees that during the evening, while he was at the pool, Gesha snooped around the house, tried to open his phone, looked through the locked room's keyhole, touched the taxidermy, and checked her own phone before turning it off and going to sleep. Nali deletes all the footage.

He opens the locked room for the first time in the film. Inside: photographs of his past life in Europe — with a man and woman who appear to be his parents. He introduces the dead Gesha to his old photos, a heartbreaking gesture of connection.

Nali drives with Gesha's body wrapped in a doll-covering. He digs a hole near overturned tankers in a desert wasteland. He tries to unlock Gesha's phone — it's dead and locked. He buys a burner phone and SIM card. But then he **changes his mind** — he can't bury her. He wraps her body and leaves it by a tree so she can be found by her family. He burns her phone and belongings but keeps her ID next to the body. He watches from a distance as an ambulance eventually finds her.

He texts AI: "The hero left the body so the family could find her." AI is offline — the "version update" his tech friend Umid warned about has happened. Nali is now truly alone.

**Scene 38 — The Theater.** Nali sits alone in an empty theater watching a rehearsal. A dancer in a red dress reminds him of Gesha. He breaks down and cancels everything.

**Scene 39 — The Police.** Officers come to Nali's old, dusty apartment and ask him to come to the station regarding both Adib's death and Gesha's disappearance.

**Scene 40 — The Interrogation.** At the station, Nali meets **Awin**, Gesha's roommate. The officer reveals the truth: Gesha died of a heart attack caused by diabetic shock from excessive sugar intake — **the Shrubi sweets**. She had diabetes. Someone who knew about her condition might have used it to kill her. But the officer implies it could also be natural — she ate the sweets herself, willingly, joyfully.

Nali realizes everything. His panic, the hiding, the two deaths — all for nothing. She died of natural causes. If he had simply called an ambulance on the morning he found her, none of this would have happened.

**Scene 41 — Awin at the Cafe.** Nali meets Awin privately. She tells him Gesha's real story: she was a runaway. Her mother died when she was young. Her father forced her to marry a cousin. After that marriage fell apart, she fell in love with a boy whose family rejected her because of her diabetes. The boy left for Europe. Gesha then left home entirely. Awin took her in. Gesha worked as a house cleaner to pay for her insulin. A salon owner offered to waive her rent if Gesha dated him — Awin advised Gesha to accept for practical survival. Instead, Gesha refused, left, and never came back. She was looking for real love, not arrangements.

Nali is shattered. He asks: "So you put the same pressure on her that her family did." It rains.

**Scene 42 — Surreal Vision.** Nali sits outside the garden house. It catches fire in his mind. Gula the dog plays in the flames. A doll-like Gesha dances in the fire.

**Scene 43 — The End.** We return to Scene 2's framing. Nali sits in the garden at sunset. The gardener finishes his story about the dead neighbors. Nali looks at his hand — the finger isn't cut. He was imagining. His phone rings. It's Gesha. Video call. She's alive. He answers, and the camera pulls back to reveal the whole garden, the house, the dog, and Nali — a man who just lived an entire nightmare in his mind. The film was his imagination of what could happen if he lets Gesha visit.

## Key Themes & Motifs

**Masks:** The central metaphor. Nali and Gesha's Scene 3 conversation about masks in society becomes the film's thesis. Everyone wears masks. The theater masks in the house are literal. The question is: what happens when they come off?

**Trust vs. Paranoia:** Nali can't trust anyone — he talks to AI instead of humans. When crisis comes, his inability to trust (calling AI instead of an ambulance) creates the catastrophe.

**The Sofa:** The most important prop. Gesha chooses to sleep on it. She dies on it. Her body is hidden inside it. Friends sit on it. Adib tries to open it. It's the physical center of the tragedy.

**Shrubi Sweets:** Chekhov's gun. Introduced in Scene 7 as nostalgia. Consumed joyfully in Scene 16. Revealed as the cause of death in Scene 40.

**The Locked Room:** Contains Nali's real identity — his European past, his parents. Gesha tries to look through the keyhole. The audience's curiosity mirrors hers. When it finally opens, it reveals not horror but tenderness.

**The Seagull:** Chekhov's play is rehearsed within the film. Its themes (failed art, unrequited love, self-destruction) mirror the main story. Adib as Trigorin. The stuffed bird becomes the seagull.

**AI as False Companion:** Nali's AI is helpful but ultimately fails him — it goes offline during the version update when he needs it most. Technology cannot replace human connection.

## Critical Notes for Revision Work

**The Twist (Scene 43):** The entire film is Nali's imagination — he's sitting in the garden during Scene 2 imagining what could happen if Gesha visits. This is powerful but risky. If handled poorly, it can feel like a cheat ("it was all a dream"). The gardener's story in Scene 2 triggers the imagined scenario. The uncut finger is the tell.

**The Shrubi Setup:** Currently subtle — possibly too subtle. The audience needs to register that Gesha craves sweets AND has a health concern, without telegraphing the reveal. The current screenplay handles this well: Gesha mentions she hasn't had Shrubi in years, implies a craving, but never explicitly says "I'm diabetic."

**Gesha's Phone:** She turns it off repeatedly, doesn't open messages, is nervous about being seen. These details pay off when we learn she's a runaway hiding from her old life.

**Pacing Concern:** The Truth or Dare sequence (Scenes 14–18) is very long. This is where producers might want compression. However, this section builds the relationship that makes the death devastating.
