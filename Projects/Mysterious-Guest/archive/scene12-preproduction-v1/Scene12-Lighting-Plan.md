## Lighting Philosophy

This scene is lit primarily by PRACTICAL lights — the pendant lamps, shelf lights, and candle-like sources that already exist in the room. The film lighting supplements, shapes, and controls what the practicals establish. The audience should feel like the room is lit by the room itself.

**The rule: If it looks lit, it's wrong.**

The mood is warm amber throughout, but not uniformly warm. There is a temperature gradient: the center of the room (dining table, kitchen area) is warmest and brightest. The edges (west wall with masks, the door area, the corners) are cooler and darker. Gesha moves from warmth to shadow and back as she explores — the light itself tells her emotional story.

### Color Palette

| Zone | Color Temperature | Feel |
|------|-------------------|------|
| Center (table, kitchen) | 2700K — deep warm amber | Safe, domestic, inviting |
| Shelf area (east) | 2700–3000K — golden | Warm curiosity, the collection |
| Back wall (curtains, deer head) | 3200K — slightly less warm | Transition zone — fading daylight |
| West wall (masks, shadows) | Cooler by absence — shadow, not blue | Unease, the unknown |
| Door area (northeast) | Dim, neutral | Boundary, the threshold she shouldn't cross |
| Window gap (faked exterior) | 3500–4000K — warm sunset with slight coolness | The outside world, fading, leaving her alone inside |

**No blue. No cool white. No daylight.** The only exception is the faked window light, which is slightly cooler than the interior to create depth and the feeling that the sun is setting *away* from this room.

## Light Sources Plan

### PRACTICALS (In-Frame Lights — Seen by Camera)

These are the lights the audience sees. They define the reality of the space.

| Source | Location | Bulb | Dimmer Setting | Purpose |
|--------|----------|------|----------------|---------|
| Pendant Lamp 1 (Main) | Center, above dining table | 2700K LED, 60W equivalent | 70–80% | Primary key for the table area. Creates the warm pool that anchors the room. Gesha puts her bags down in this light. |
| Pendant Lamp 2 | Above kitchen/shelf area (east) | 2700K LED, 40W equivalent | 60–70% | Lights Nali when he's at the kitchen counter. Creates his warm halo in the OTS shot (12.4). |
| Pendant Lamp 3 | Near back wall area | 2700K LED, 40W equivalent | 40–50% | Dimmer than the others — the back of the room should feel further away. Provides fill on the curtains. |
| Shelf Lights | East wall shelving (existing) | Existing warm LEDs/candle bulbs | As-is or slightly dimmed | The golden glow on the glassware and pottery. These create depth and texture on the east side. Don't touch if they look good. |
| Desk Lamp (to add) | On/near the desk area | Small warm LED, 2700K | Low — enough to read the papers | A working lamp on Nali's desk. Adds realism. Lights Gesha's face from below-left in Shot 12.11. |

**Critical fix:** The LED strip on the back wall (visible in reference photos, cool white ~5000K) must be TURNED OFF. It destroys the warm palette and creates an ugly cool wash on the deer head and curtains. If the strip is needed for general illumination during setup, turn it off before rolling.

### FILM LIGHTS (Off-Camera — Shaping and Supplementing)

These are the lights the audience never sees. They solve problems the practicals can't.

| Unit | Type | Position | Purpose | Settings |
|------|------|----------|---------|----------|
| LED Panel 1 (Key Fill) | Bi-color LED panel, 1×1 or equivalent | East side of room, high, angled down and across toward center. Mounted on C-stand, boomed over the shelf area. | Provides a gentle warm fill for the center of the room. Prevents the practicals from creating TOO much contrast — faces need to be readable. | 2800K, 20–30% intensity. Diffused through 1/2 grid cloth or bounced off a white card. SOFT. If you can identify this light on screen, it's too strong. |
| LED Panel 2 (Back/Edge) | Bi-color LED panel, smaller unit | Outside the window, aimed through the curtain gap | Fakes the dying evening sunlight. Creates a warm streak across the floor and back wall. Justifies the "evening" setting regardless of actual shoot time. | 3500–4000K, 40–60%. Bare (no diffusion) — direct sun through a gap should be slightly harsh, not soft. CTO 1/2 gel if the unit doesn't go warm enough. |
| Dedolight / Small Fresnel | Focusable spot, 150W or LED equivalent | Floor level, angled UP at the deer head from below-right (~1.5m away) | Creates the GLINT in the deer head's glass eyes and the dramatic upward shadows on its face and antlers. This is the deer head's "life." | Focused tight (spot position), barndoored to keep light off the curtains. 3000K. Intensity: medium — the glint should be a pinpoint, not a flood. |
| Battery LED (Dim Practical) | Small battery-powered LED, warm | Hidden behind bookshelf edge on west wall, aimed at masks | Creates the faintest illumination on the masks — just enough to see their pale forms. The masks should be BARELY visible until Gesha discovers them. | 2700K, absolute minimum intensity. If the masks are fully lit, the reveal is ruined. Test by squinting — you should barely see them. |
| Optional: LED Tube / Wand | Handheld LED wand, bi-color | Held off-camera by grip as needed | Mobile fill for Gesha as she moves through the space. Used only if her face falls into unreadable shadow during her walking path. | 2700K, very low. Handheld by a grip who follows her movement off-camera. Only if needed — the practicals should do most of the work. |

## Shot-by-Shot Lighting Notes

### Shot 12.1 — Tracking Shot (Setup A)

| Element | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| Primary Light | Pendant Lamp 1 (table) — Gesha walks INTO this light as she enters |
| Secondary | Pendant Lamp 2 (kitchen) — Nali walks toward this light |
| Fill | LED Panel 1 at low intensity — prevents Gesha from going full silhouette at the entrance |
| Back Light | LED Panel 2 (window) — warm streak visible in background |
| Challenge | The camera moves 3m forward — the lighting must work across the ENTIRE room depth, not just one spot. Test the full dolly path and check exposure at entrance (darker) vs. mid-room (brighter). |
| DP Note | Consider exposing for Gesha's face at her STOP position (mid-room) and letting the entrance be slightly darker. The pull from dark to warm is the visual story of crossing a threshold. |

### Shot 12.2 — Low Angle Room Reveal (Setup B)

| Element | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| Primary Light | ALL practicals on — this is the beauty shot of the room |
| Deer Head | Dedolight on, creating the dramatic upward-lit deer head |
| Masks | Dim practical barely showing the pale shapes |
| Shelves | Shelf lights creating golden glow |
| Challenge | The 18mm ultra-wide will see EVERYTHING, including light stands if they're in the wrong place. Strike or reposition all film lights that might creep into frame edges. |
| DP Note | This shot defines the audience's mental map of the room. Every light source should be on and looking its best. Consider this the "album cover" of the location. |

### Shot 12.3 — Close-up Gesha (Setup C)

| Element | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| Key | Pendant Lamp 1 from above-right creates Rembrandt pattern on her face |
| Fill | LED Panel 1 bounced, very soft, from left — just enough to see the shadow side of her face |
| Hair/Rim | Pendant lamp glow creates a warm edge on her curly hair (camera-left side) |
| Eyes | CRITICAL — catchlights must appear in both eyes. The pendant lamp above-right creates the primary catchlight. If the second eye is dead, add the faintest bounce card (white, held low, camera-left) to create a secondary. |
| DP Note | 85mm at wide aperture — background becomes a warm blur of amber shapes. This blur IS the aesthetic. Don't sharpen the background by stopping down — let it sing. |

### Shot 12.4 — OTS Nali (Setup D)

| Element | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| Nali (foreground) | Near-silhouette. Lit from behind by Pendant Lamp 2 and shelf lights — edge light on shoulder and beard profile. Face mostly in shadow. |
| Gesha (background) | Lit by Pendant Lamp 1 and LED Panel 1 fill — she should be the brightest element in frame despite being further away. |
| DP Note | The exposure is set for GESHA in the background. Nali goes dark naturally. This creates the watcher/watched dynamic through light alone. |

### Shot 12.5 — POV Deer Head (Setup E)

| Element | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| Hero Light | Dedolight focused on deer head — the glass eye GLINT is the shot. |
| Ambient | Pendant Lamp 3 provides general background wash. All other practicals create the room behind camera. |
| Challenge | The camera pushes IN — the lighting on the deer head must look consistent from wide to close. The dedolight angle must not create a shadow from the antlers that shifts noticeably during the push. Test the full slider travel. |
| DP Note | The slight handheld drift means the glint in the eye may flicker as the camera angle shifts — this is GOOD. It makes the eye feel alive. Don't stabilize it away. |

### Shot 12.6 — Two Shot Dialogue (Setup F)

| Element | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| Gesha (right) | Pendant Lamp 1 key from above. Slightly more shadow on her — she's further from the fill. |
| Nali (left) | Pendant Lamp 2 key + shelf glow. Warmer and more evenly lit than Gesha. |
| Background | Deer head visible between them — dedolight still on, creating the pin of light. |
| DP Note | The LIGHT tells the power dynamic: Nali is bathed in warm, even light (comfortable, in control). Gesha has more shadow on her face (uncertain, exposed). This is not accidental — rig it this way deliberately. |

### Shots 12.7, 12.9 — Gesha at Masks (Setup G)

| Element | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| Gesha | Walking from warm center into cooler edge. As she approaches the masks, the warm key (Pendant 1) falls off and she's lit more by ambient bounce. Her face should dim slightly. |
| Masks | Dim battery LED barely reveals them. As Gesha gets closer, the mask area doesn't get brighter — SHE brings herself into the dark, the dark doesn't come to the light. |
| DP Note | Resist the urge to add fill here. The dimming is the point. The masks live in shadow. |

### Shot 12.8 — Mask Insert (Setup H)

| Element | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| Hero Light | Reposition the dedolight (or use a second small unit) to floor level, aimed UP at the masks from below-right. Creates horror-movie uplighting in the eye sockets. |
| Ambient | Near zero. The masks should pop against near-darkness. |
| DP Note | The Dutch angle + uplighting = maximum unease from minimum means. One light, one angle, done. |

### Shots 12.10, 12.12 — Nali's Coverage (Setup I)

| Element | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| Key | Pendant Lamp 2 + shelf glow — same as 12.4 and 12.6 but now he's the subject, not the background. |
| Fill | LED Panel 1 or a bounce card from camera-left — just enough to read his eyes. |
| 12.12 (Close-up) | Tighter framing naturally darkens the edges. His eyes must have catchlights. The moment before he answers — his eyes shift — the audience needs to SEE that micro-movement. |
| DP Note | Same warm, comfortable light as before. Nali doesn't change — his light is consistent because HE is consistent. The house is his environment. |

### Shot 12.11 — Desk Discovery (Setup J)

| Element | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| Gesha | Lit by the desk lamp (practical, in frame) from below-left. This creates an uplight quality on her face — slightly unusual, slightly unsettling. She's reading something she wasn't supposed to find. |
| Paper Insert | The desk lamp must illuminate the "Stories are waiting" text clearly for the close-up insert. Angle the lamp so the paper surface doesn't reflect glare into the lens. Matte paper helps. |
| Laptop | If screen is on, it becomes a second light source — cool blue-white. Consider whether this mixed temperature adds to the unease or distracts. Recommendation: screen on but dim, showing unreadable text. The slight cool glow on Gesha's chin contrasts with the warm room. |
| DP Note | This is the one shot where the lighting is noticeably different — desk lamp uplight instead of pendant downlight. It should feel like she's stepped into a private space, lit differently because it IS different. |

### Shot 12.13 — Return to Deer (Setup K)

| Element | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| Gesha | Returns to the warm center. Her face re-enters the Pendant Lamp 1 key. After the darker mask area and desk uplight, the return to warm even light should feel like a release. |
| Deer Head | Dedolight still holding steady. The deer is lit exactly as before — it hasn't changed. She has. |
| DP Note | The pan following Gesha tracks her from cooler to warmer light. This mirrors her emotional shift — from suspicion to engagement. She's warming up. Literally. |

### Shot 12.14 — Locked Door (Setup L)

| Element | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| Gesha (foreground) | Moving into the dimmest part of the room — the door area has no dedicated practical. She walks OUT of the warm center into relative darkness. |
| Nali (background) | Standing in the warm center, lit by Pendants 1 and 2. He stays in the light while she goes to the dark. |
| The Doors | Minimal illumination — just enough to see the door handles and distinguish the two doors. If too dark for the camera, add a tiny practical (a wall sconce or LED hidden behind the door frame) at absolute minimum intensity. |
| DP Note | The lighting geography IS the story. Warm center = safety. Dark edges = danger. She walks toward the locked door and leaves the light behind. Nali stays lit. The wide framing (28mm) captures this gradient across the full room. This is the most important lighting setup of the scene. |

### Shot 12.15 — Nali Alone (Setup M)

| Element | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| Nali | Standing in center of room. Pendant Lamp 1 overhead but he's not directly under it — he's slightly offset, so the light catches one side of his face more than the other. More shadow than any previous Nali shot. |
| Deer Head (background) | Dedolight still on. One antler, one glass eye. The deer watches him from behind. |
| The Empty Room (held after Nali exits) | When Nali leaves frame, the camera holds on the ROOM. The lights stay. The warm amber pools, the dark corners, the glint in the deer's eye. The house is lit as if someone is still home, but no one is. |
| DP Note | Consider dimming Pendant Lamp 1 by 5–10% from its earlier setting. The audience may not consciously register it, but the room will feel SUBTLY emptier, colder. The warmth is leaving with the people. Or — leave it exactly the same, and the emptiness is created by the absence of faces in the light. Director's choice. Discuss on set. |

## Pre-Shoot Light Check Protocol

On the day before (pre-dress) or morning of:

1. **Turn on ALL practicals only — no film lights.** Walk through the room and photograph from every camera position. This is your BASELINE — the room as the audience would see it in reality.

2. **Add film lights one at a time.** LED Panel 1 first. Photograph. Then dedolight on deer head. Photograph. Then window LED. Photograph. Each addition should enhance, not overpower, the practicals.

3. **Do the squint test.** Stand where the camera goes. Squint at the room. If a film light is obviously brighter than the practicals, it's too bright. Dim it.

4. **Check every shot's framing** for light stands, cables, bounce cards, or diffusion frames in frame. Especially Shot 12.2 (18mm ultra-wide sees everything) and Shot 12.14 (28mm wide).

5. **Photograph the final lighting state** from every camera position. These are your REFERENCE photos for continuity if anything gets bumped, dimmed, or moved during the day.
