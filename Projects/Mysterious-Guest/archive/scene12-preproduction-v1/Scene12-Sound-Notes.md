## Sound Philosophy

Scene 12 is a dialogue scene inside a quiet house at evening. The sound design has two layers: what's RECORDED on set (dialogue, footsteps, practical sounds), and what's BUILT in post (ambient atmosphere, subtle design elements). The goal on set is to capture clean dialogue and rich practical sounds. Everything else can be layered later — but bad production sound cannot be fixed.

**The golden rule for this scene: silence is the third actor.** Between the lines of dialogue, the audience should hear the house breathing — the hum of the space, a creak, the stillness. These silences are not dead air. They are the sound of Gesha being alone in a strange place with a man she barely knows.

| Lavalier Mic — Nali (wireless) | Safety track for Nali's dialogue. Hidden under shirt collar or in beard area. | Transmitter pack taped to lower back or belt. Antenna routing away from body. | Lavalier Mic — Gesha (wireless) | Safety track for Gesha's dialogue. Hidden in neckline or hair near collar. | Curly hair can brush against the mic capsule and create rustling. | Portable Recorder (e.g., Sound Devices MixPre or Zoom F6) | Multi-track recording: Track 1 = boom, Track 2 = lav Nali, Track 3 = lav Gesha, Track 4 = room tone / ambient mic. | Record at 48kHz / 24-bit. Timecode sync with camera if available. If no timecode, use scratch audio on camera + clapper for sync. |

## Post-Production Sound Design Notes

These are notes for the sound designer / editor. They describe the INTENDED sound world of Scene 12 — what should be built AFTER production wrapping, using the recorded material plus design elements.

### Ambient Bed

The room has a PRESENCE — a low, warm hum that sits under everything. Not music, not a recognizable sound. More like the tone of a space that holds secrets. Build from:
- Room tone recordings
- Very low frequency pad (20–40Hz, barely audible, felt more than heard)
- A subtle, occasional creak (wooden shelf settling, house breathing)

This ambient bed should be BELOW the dialogue threshold. If the audience notices it, it's too loud. If you remove it, the scene should feel colder and thinner.

### Key Sound Design Moments

| Moment | Design Direction |
|--------|-----------------|
| Gesha enters (12.1) | The ambient bed begins as she crosses the threshold. Before entry = exterior quiet. After entry = the room's frequency takes over. The transition should be gradual (over 2–3 seconds), not a hard cut. |
| Deer head POV (12.5) | The ambient drops to near-silence. A very faint high-frequency tone (tinnitus-like, barely there) suggests Gesha's unease. When the push-in reaches its closest point and the eye-glint is visible, the tone fades and we're left with pure room silence. |
| Mask discovery (12.7, 12.8) | Consider a barely perceptible LOW-END swell under the mask insert (12.8). Not a musical sting — more like the room exhaling. A pressure change. |
| "Stories are waiting" paper (12.11) | The paper pickup sound should be slightly ISOLATED — a tiny dip in the ambient bed, making the paper crinkle feel louder and more significant than it naturally is. The audience's subconscious: "This matters." |
| Locked door handle (12.14) | The handle click/resistance should be SLIGHTLY louder than natural. Not cartoonishly so — maybe 2–3 dB above where it would naturally sit. The ambient bed dips 1 dB as Gesha reaches for the handle, then returns after Nali's line. The audience won't notice the dip. Their subconscious will. |
| Empty room (end of 12.15) | After Nali exits, the ambient bed is alone. No footsteps, no breathing, no voice. Just the house. Hold for 3–5 seconds. The room hums. The deer head watches. Then cut. |

### Music Notes

Confirm with director: does this scene have score/music, or is it purely sound design? Recommendation: NO MUSIC in Scene 12. The scene is about the strangeness of a real place, not a stylized one. Music tells the audience how to feel. Sound design lets them discover it themselves. If the director insists on music, recommend it enter only at the very end (12.15, empty room) — a single sustained tone or a faint string harmonic that carries into Scene 13.

## On-Set Sound Checklist

### Before First Take
- [ ] Room tone recorded (60 seconds, full silence)
- [ ] Noise sources identified and addressed (fridge unplugged, HVAC off, phones on silent)
- [ ] Boom tested for reach in every shooting position (especially tracking shot — can boom op keep up with dolly?)
- [ ] Lavs tested on both actors — fabric rustle check (especially Nali's linen shirt, Gesha's hair)
- [ ] Lav placement photographed for continuity
- [ ] Recorder levels set — test with actors speaking at performance volume from performance distance
- [ ] Timecode sync confirmed (or clapper slate verified)
- [ ] Crew briefed: no talking, no phone vibrations, no shifting during takes. This scene has SILENCES that are as important as dialogue.

### During Shooting
- [ ] Mixer confirms "clean" or flags issues immediately after each take — do NOT wait until review
- [ ] Any aircraft, car horn, or dog bark during a take = mark as potential issue on sound report
- [ ] Monitor lav tracks for fabric rustle — adjust between takes if needed
- [ ] Log take numbers with notes: "clean," "plane at 0:12," "lav rustle on Gesha," etc.

### After Wrap
- [ ] Wild sound takes recorded (door handle, footsteps, room tone, ambience — see list above)
- [ ] Exterior ambience recorded (2 min minimum)
- [ ] All audio files backed up to two separate drives before leaving location
- [ ] Sound report completed with take notes, channel assignments, and any known issues
