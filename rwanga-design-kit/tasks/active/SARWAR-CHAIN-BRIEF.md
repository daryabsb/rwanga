## What is a chain?

A chain is a test. It takes a group of decisions from your review and asks: **do these decisions actually work together across scenes, or are they just sitting next to each other on a list?**

Here's what I mean. Right now, your v03 review has 19 unsettled decisions — each one asking a question about whether something in the story works. Things like: "Is Gesha's death mechanism believable?" or "Is the couch overdone as a symbol?" or "Does the ending land?"

These decisions look independent. But they're not. They form chains — groups where settling one decision forces you to settle the others, because they share the same structural logic across multiple scenes.

**Example — plain language:**

Take the sherbet. You have an unsettled decision asking: "Is the hint ('these will kill me') enough for the audience, or do we need more foreshadowing?"

But you can't answer that alone. Because the sherbet connects to Gesha's diabetes (another unsettled decision), which connects to how her death works (another), which connects to what Evin reveals at the police station (another). If you decide the hint is enough, you're also deciding the diabetes reveal can stay late. If you decide the hint needs strengthening, you might need Evin to appear earlier. One decision pulls the others.

That's a chain. The decisions are load-bearing for each other.

---

## What does building a chain achieve?

Three things:

**1. It forces honest answers.** When you look at a decision in isolation — "Is the couch overdone?" — you can say "it's fine" and move on. But when you see the couch connected to the locked room connected to the house-as-system, all three sharing the same structural job (the physical world encodes Nali's control), now "it's fine" isn't enough. You have to say what it's actually doing and whether the three together are doing too much, too little, or the right amount.

**2. It shows you the real dependencies.** Some decisions feel separate but aren't. The opening montage and the final twist look like they live at opposite ends of the film, but they're the same structural mechanism (flash-forward fragments that only make sense at the end). If you settle the twist without thinking about the montage, you might create a contradiction.

**3. It validates the lens format.** We've been building analytical lenses — short English summaries that define the rule each scene obeys. The D15 mediation chain (which you already reviewed with the consultant) proved the format works for locked decisions. Now we test it on unsettled ones. If it works here too, we have the format for the entire interactive bible viewer.

---

## Why pick one chain first?

Because doing all four at once would take days and produce shallow work. One chain, done properly, teaches us the method. The second chain goes faster. By the fourth, we'll have the entire v03 review resolved — not decision by decision, but structurally, the way the film actually works.

Also: your feedback on the first chain will shape how we build the rest. If you say "the connections are too forced" or "you're missing a scene," we adjust before repeating the process.
