Premise

Three verbs, and a refusal

The Scene Sidebar Catalogue is orientation, navigation, and awareness. Three verbs, no more. Where am I in the structure? Take me to that scene. How much script is there, and what's near me? It belongs to the UX Direction's Session Awareness layer — "peripheral, you glance, you don't stare." The screenplay page is the heaviest object in the room and the permanent center of gravity. The catalogue orbits it.

Observation — the anti-pattern in the current shell

The screenshot's breakdown accordion — CAST (5), PROPS (8), STUNTS (14), ADD NEW CATEGORY — is a database-table interface. Counts-in-parentheses is inventory thinking. As the default writing sidebar it makes a writing tool feel like a production database with a text field attached. That surface belongs in breakdown mode (§16); the scene catalogue belongs in the writer's hands.

Doctrine anchor

It is not management. Not a production inventory. Not a dashboard. The catalogue earns its calm by refusing most of what could be shown. Every dimension below is, at root, a discipline against accretion.

01 · Row Anatomy

The row is a label, not a card

The scene row is the atomic unit of the whole surface; everything else is frame. A row encodes three zones in one glance and one line, reading inline-start → inline-end: an index gutter (the number, fixed-width, monospaced — numbers stack into a scannable column), a heading (the slug, the one load-bearing token, flexing and ellipsising), and a signal margin (awareness marks + page hint, in fixed reserved tracks that never reflow the heading).

Direction

Label rows scan; card rows demand. Keep the row a label. Anything that makes a row hold content rather than point at it is the admin panel knocking.

Direction

Never make current louder to "fix" its visibility. Its problem is positional — it scrolls off-screen (§14) — not chromatic. Keep it on-screen; never make it brighter.

04 · Scene Numbering

A typographic column, not a label

  • A column. Right-aligned, monospaced (Courier — the page's screenplay DNA), in --accent-gold, the warmth accent reserved for structural signals. Numbers stack into a scannable edge.
  • Display, never compute. Numbers come from nav-index (scene.sceneNumber), which the engine owns. The catalogue renders numbers; it never assigns or renumbers. Locked by the nav-index moratorium.
  • Drop the decorative #. The fixed gutter already says "this is the number." A literal # adds noise to every row.
  • Plan for non-integer numbers. Production numbering (A1, 12A) is a real future — right-align and tolerate ~3–4 mono chars now, so the gutter never has to be re-laid-out. (Generation of such numbers is deferred and engine-owned.)
  • Honest blanks. A pre-scene / title sequence renders an empty or muted gutter — never a faked "0."

05 · Page / Location Indicators

One coordinate, and a refusal to color the rows

Page

The most useful awareness number after the scene number. The page where the scene starts (p.3), right-aligned, monospaced, dim. One number, not a range, at rest — a span can reveal on hover/inspect. It is awareness, not pagination control: no "go to page" lives here. Shown only when PageMap data exists; otherwise the track stays reserved-but-empty (no "p.?" noise).

Location

There is no separate location indicator — location is already the heaviest token in the heading. A chip or swatch would duplicate the slug and reintroduce inventory texture.

Direction

Resist location color-coding. Tinting rows by place is the single most tempting slide toward the breakdown panel — it turns a calm list into a heat-map and demands a legend. Location lives as text, where it's already legible.

08 · Icon Strategy

A deliberately tiny budget

Rwanga's identity is restraint — VS Code calm, explicitly anti-Discord, anti-AI-playground.

  • No per-row leading icons. INT./EXT. is text, and text is clearer and more screenplay-native than a door/tree glyph. Time-of-day is NIGHT, not a moon.
  • One family, line weight, sized to text. Where icons appear — panel identity, the note/revision marks, a collapse chevron if grouping ships — they come from the single Lucide set: line-style, monochrome. No filled tiles, no two-tone.
  • Never emoji. No place in a professional writing instrument's chrome.
  • Marks vs icons. Treat the note/revision signals as typographic marks — the restraint of punctuation, not the prominence of buttons.

  • Quiet section identity matching the established .sidebar-section-header voice — small, uppercase, wide-tracked.
  • One awareness figure, used with caution. A scene count (Scenes · 42) is genuine orientation — but it's one keystroke from feeling like a tally. If it reads as inventory, drop it. One number, never a row of stats.

10 · Search / Filter Posture

Find, not query

The catalogue's search exists to jump, not to build saved, faceted views.

  • A single progressive find field — understated, expands on focus; substring-matches the visible slug and the scene number. Solves "find the dancer scene" without scrolling or the palette.
  • Temporary, not sticky. Escape/blur clears it and restores the full list. The catalogue never persists a filtered state as a saved view — that's a management construct.
  • No faceted filtering. "All night exteriors," filter-by-tag — those are breakdown queries (§16). At most one optional presence toggle ("scenes with notes").
  • Honor the existing power path. Cmd+P quick-scene-jump is the keyboard-first jump; the find field is its discoverable sibling. Complementary, not redundant.
  • Escape precedence is explicit: first Escape clears the filter, second clears selection.

11 · Empty State

Calm instruction, never a marketing surface

  • No scenes yet — quiet, centered, --text-3: one sentence on what the catalogue will show. Describe the purpose ("Scenes will appear here as you write"), not the keystroke — doc-type-neutral as the platform grows.
  • No results — "No scenes match '…'" with a one-tap clear-filter affordance, so the writer is never stranded.
  • No illustration, no CTA hero. A zero-state graphic or "Create your first scene" button is dashboard onboarding — the admin-panel impression to avoid.

12 · Density Rules

A comfort dial, not a feature surface

  • 28px is the calm default — right for a feature script (~40–90 scenes), matching the chrome-spacing philosophy.
  • Adaptive, single-token. A long episodic (200+ short scenes) benefits from a tighter row — one compact step governed by one density token, never per-element nudges.
  • What compresses, in order: Tier-2 modifiers yield first — time-of-day, then the INT./EXT. abbreviation. The number gutter, location, and reserved signal margin never compress away.
  • Fixed margins across densities, so the no-reflow guarantee survives.
Direction

Density changes how much air a row has — never what the row means.

13 · Hover / Focus Behavior

Three layers, no nervous reflow

Hover (transient mouse), selected (intent), and current (cursor) must coexist without muddiness.

  • Hover is a whisper — a subtle --bg-hover lift and slight brightening, nothing structural. No buttons appear, no card expands. The one acceptable reveal: an OS tooltip for a truncated slug.
  • No layout shift, ever. Fills, rails, and rings draw on pre-reserved tracks (§1). The difference between a surface that feels solid and one that feels nervous.
  • Focus is keyboard-first and complete — Arrow moves selection without moving the cursor; Home/End jump; Enter bridges selection → cursor; Escape clears. Protect this model; keep the ring distinct from hover and current.
  • Click navigates and confirms — the existing ~1.2s flash pulse answers "did it work?" without a modal. Keep it brief.
  • Power actions live behind right-click, never as hover-revealed buttons that fight the scan.

14 · Writer Workflow Support

Orient → navigate → return to writing

Observation — the dominant lived weakness

The writer types in scene 47 of a 60-scene script while the panel shows scenes 1–25 — so the pink "you are here" marker points off-screen. The catalogue claims awareness while delivering none.

  • Auto-scroll the current row into view — the spine. When the current scene changes, the current row must come into view. The highest-value single behavior the surface can gain. It must be functional, not theatrical: instant alignment, no decorative motion, fired only on a real transition and only when the row is off-screen.
  • Click-to-jump + flash confirm is the navigation core. Keep it.
  • Keyboard jump (Cmd+P) is the power path; the find field (§10) the discoverable one.
  • Act/sequence grouping is deferred, not directed — it would genuinely help long scripts but requires new per-scene data, which collides with the nav-index moratorium. Gate it behind that work.
  • Reordering does NOT belong here. Drag-to-reorder is a structural edit — management. It belongs to outline/breakdown. The catalogue's gestures are click, keyboard, right-click. No drag handles.
  • Never add completion checkboxes, status toggles, assignment, or color-by-status — each a small, reasonable-sounding step toward the dashboard.

15 · 16 · What Belongs Elsewhere

The cleanest test: which scene, vs. what's inside it

The catalogue answers "which scene, and where am I?" The inspector answers "what's inside this scene?" Breakdown mode answers "what does production need across all scenes?"

§15 — To the Inspector
  • Notes content (the row shows only a presence mark)
  • Tagged entities for the scene (characters, props, locations)
  • Revision history & flag detail
  • Scene metadata editing (rename location, change time-of-day)
  • Scene-level AI observations (gated behind Alive App)
  • Estimated duration, when a model exists
§16 — To Breakdown Mode
  • The category accordion — CAST / PROPS / STUNTS counts
  • ADD NEW CATEGORY & category CMS actions
  • Cross-scene queries ("every scene with this character")
  • Per-category counts and tallies
  • Bulk ops, scheduling, stripboard, status tracking
Direction — the central decision

The two surfaces share the same physical sidebar zone but never the same default: writing → scene catalogue; breakdown work → breakdown panel (entered deliberately from the rail). This separation is what keeps the editor feeling like a writing tool rather than a production database with a text field attached. The instant a row becomes editable in place or expandable into a detail card, the inspector's reason to exist evaporates.

Closing

01

Strongest current behavior

The current-vs-selected SEPARATION INVARIANT. The catalogue already distinguishes current (cursor position — passive, brand-pink edge rail, tied to ScriptSession.currentScene) from selected (keyboard intent — soft fill + ring) and lets them coexist on one row without collapsing into a single ambiguous "active" state. It separates where you are from what you're looking at — the mature, correct anatomy for a writing-first navigator. Documented in code, enforced in CSS, pinned by tests. Build on it; never erode it.

02

Most important UX change

Establish the scene catalogue as the sidebar's primary writing surface, and make "you are here" actually visible by keeping the current row on-screen. Two faces of one principle: the catalogue must be the default writing companion (not the breakdown accordion, which belongs in breakdown mode), and it must deliver the awareness it promises. Today the pink marker points off-screen the moment the writer moves past the visible rows. Auto-scrolling the current row into view converts the catalogue from a static list into a live "you are here" map — repairing the surface's core promise at its weakest point.

03

Safest first implementation slice

Auto-scroll the current row into view when the current scene changes (SN.1). Pure presentation behavior, isolated to the navigator: touches no engine code, honors the nav-index moratorium (no new per-scene data), changes no CSS identity, and preserves the separation invariant, keyboard nav, click-to-jump, and empty state untouched. It must align instantly (no theatrical motion), fire only on a real current-scene transition, and only when the row is actually off-screen. Small, reversible, and it fixes the most-felt weakness — the correct first step before any visual-refinement slice.

04

Highest-risk change to avoid

Letting the catalogue become a management surface. Per-scene tag/character counts, duration columns, status toggles or checkboxes, location color-coding, drag-to-reorder, faceted/saved filters, or promoting the breakdown accordion back into the writing default — each pulls the surface toward the admin-panel/dashboard the Doctrine rejects, and most also collide with the nav-index moratorium. The subtler trap: making current louder to "fix" its visibility when the real problem is positional, solved by scrolling. Guard the row's calm; it is a spent budget, not a canvas.

STOP

UX Direction only. No implementation has begun, no code has been written, and no engineering plan beyond the single recommended first slice has been authored. The numbering, indexing, and schema constraints (the nav-index moratorium, engine freeze) are treated as immovable. The next decision — authorize, amend, or reject — belongs to the user.
