First: what is the "page"?

Your screenshot doesn't read as a script because there is no page — just uppercase text on a dark void. Before scene lines, action, dialogue, or the "+" buttons, we have to decide what surface they all sit on. Here are the three real options. Each card shows the same scene rendered in that model.

1.
INT. CAFÉ - NIGHT
A dimly lit café. Rain streaks the windows. SARAH sits alone with a watch.
SARAH
(checking the time)
I've been waiting for an hour.
AHMED enters, shaking off the rain.
CUT TO:

A. Literal paper page

White US-Letter page floating on a grey desk, drop shadow, hard 1.5″/1″ margins, fixed measure, visible page breaks & page numbers. Final Draft / Celtx / Arc Studio. What a director expects to hand to a 1st AD.

B. Continuous column

Correct screenplay indentation, but no page boundary — text runs the full editor width, no shadow, no page breaks on screen. WriterDuet "endless" mode. Fast to write in; does not look like a printed script.

C. Width-constrained column

Fixed page measure (same line length as paper) on the editor background — no white page, no shadow — with a faint dashed line marking where a page break falls. Looks structured, theme-aware, still "in the IDE".

My recommendation: A — the literal paper page. You asked "would a director use this?" — a director judges a script by whether it looks like a script. Paged WYSIWYG is the one model where what's on screen is exactly what prints and what gets handed out. B and C are writer-comfort optimizations that sacrifice the thing you're angry about. But this is your call — tell me which, or describe a reference app you trust.
